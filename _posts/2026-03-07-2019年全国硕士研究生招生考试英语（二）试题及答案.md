Finland consistently ranks among the top countries in global education assessments, yet its system looks very different from those of its competitors. There are no standardized tests until the end of high school. Teachers are highly respected and required to hold master's degrees. The school day is shorter, and homework is minimal.

The Finnish philosophy centers on equity and trust. Every child, regardless of background, attends the same type of school. There is no tracking or streaming by ability at an early age. The belief is that all children can learn if given the right support. Teachers are granted significant autonomy to design their curriculum and assess students without external pressure.

Critics argue that the Finnish model relies heavily on the country's homogeneous culture and small population, making it hard to replicate elsewhere. However, educators point out that the core principles—valuing teachers, focusing on equity, and trusting professionals—are universal.

Recently, Finland has introduced phenomena-based learning, where students study real-world topics like "the European Union" or "climate change" rather than traditional subjects like history or physics in isolation. This approach aims to develop critical thinking and problem-solving skills needed for the future.

While no system is perfect, Finland's success challenges the notion that more testing and longer hours lead to better outcomes. It suggests that quality, trust, and well-being are the true keys to educational excellence.

#### Text 2 (英国皇家邮政的困境)

The Royal Mail, Britain's historic postal service, is facing an existential crisis. Once a monopoly, it now competes with private courier companies and digital communication. Its profits have plummeted, and its pension deficit is massive.

The government privatized parts of the Royal Mail to inject capital and improve efficiency. However, the transition has been rocky. Unions argue that privatization has led to job cuts and worse conditions for workers. Customers complain about delayed deliveries and rising prices.

A major issue is the universal service obligation (USO), which requires the Royal Mail to deliver to every address in the UK six days a week at a uniform price. This is increasingly costly as letter volumes drop due to email and social media. While parcel delivery is growing thanks to e-commerce, it is not enough to offset the losses from letters.

Some propose abolishing the USO or reducing delivery days to save money. Others suggest greater state subsidies to maintain the service as a public good. The debate highlights the tension between commercial viability and public service duties.

Without a clear solution, the future of the Royal Mail remains uncertain. It must adapt to the digital age while fulfilling its historic role of connecting the nation.

Defenders of the tech giants argue that their size allows them to invest heavily in research and development, benefiting society. They claim that the market is still competitive and that consumers can switch to alternatives if they are unhappy. They warn that over-regulation could stifle the very innovation that drives progress.

The outcome of this battle will shape the future of the digital economy. Finding a balance between fostering innovation and ensuring fair competition is a complex but necessary challenge.

Addressing this crisis requires bold policy interventions. Without action, the dream of homeownership may become a privilege for the few, further dividing society along generational and economic lines.
