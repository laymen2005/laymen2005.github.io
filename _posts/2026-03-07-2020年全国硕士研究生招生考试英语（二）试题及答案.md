#### Text 1 (科学出版模式的变革)

For decades, the traditional model of scientific publishing has been under fire. In this system, researchers submit their papers to journals, where they are peer-reviewed for free. If accepted, the journals publish the articles and charge libraries or individuals high subscription fees to access them. Critics argue that this model restricts the flow of knowledge and profits publishers at the expense of the public.

In recent years, the "open access" movement has gained momentum. Under this model, articles are made freely available to everyone online immediately upon publication. The costs of publishing are often covered by fees paid by the authors or their institutions, rather than by readers. Proponents say this democratizes science, allowing anyone with an internet connection to read the latest research.

However, the transition is not without challenges. Some worry that the author-pays model could disadvantage researchers from poorer institutions or developing countries who cannot afford the fees. Others question the quality control in some open-access journals, noting that the pressure to publish more papers to collect fees might compromise rigorous peer review.

Despite these concerns, major funding agencies and governments are pushing for open access. They argue that publicly funded research should be publicly available. As the debate continues, the landscape of scientific communication is shifting towards greater transparency and accessibility.
