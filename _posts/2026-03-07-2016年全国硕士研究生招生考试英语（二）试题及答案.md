#### Text 2 (草原鸡保护争议)

Biologists estimate that as many as 2 million lesser prairie chickens—a kind of bird living on stretching grasslands—once lent red to the often gray landscape of the midwestern and southwestern United States. But just some 22,000 birds remain today, occupying about 16% of the species' historic range.

The crash was a major reason the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) decided to formally list the bird as threatened. "The lesser prairie chicken is in a desperate situation," said USFWS Director Daniel Ashe. Some environmentalists, however, were disappointed. They had pushed the agency to designate the bird as "endangered," a status that gives federal officials greater regulatory power to crack down on threats. But Ashe and others argued that the "threatened" tag gave the federal government flexibility to try out new, potentially less confrontational conservation approaches. In particular, they called for forging closer collaborations with western state governments, which are often uneasy with federal action, and with the private landowners who control an estimated 95% of the prairie chicken's habitat.

Under the plan, for example, the agency said it would not prosecute landowners or businesses that unintentionally kill, harm, or disturb the bird, as long as they had signed a range-wide management plan to restore prairie chicken habitat. Negotiated by USFWS and the states, the plan requires individuals and businesses that damage habitat as part of their operations to pay into a fund to replace every acre destroyed with 2 new acres. This fund will also be used to compensate landowners who set aside habitat. The plan also sets interim population goals: increasing the bird's numbers to at least 67,000 over the next 10 years. And it provides for monitoring the progress of the recovery.

Ashe said he believed the plan would allow states to remain in the driver's seat for managing the species. "We're looking at a model where the states retain primary responsibility," he said.

Not everyone buys the win-win rhetoric. Some Congress members, environmental groups, and even some scientists are challenging the plan. Jay Lininger, a biologist with WildEarth Guardians, an environmental group, criticized the government for handing management of the species to the same industries that helped drive the bird to the brink of extinction. "The federal government is giving responsibility for managing the bird to the same industries that are pushing it to the brink of extinction," Lininger said.

#### Text 3 (深度阅读与时间管理)

That everyone's too busy these days is a cliché. But one specific complaint is made especially mournfully: There's never any time to read.

What makes the problem thornier is that the usual time-management techniques don't seem to work. If you've ever tried to squeeze reading into your schedule, you know the feeling: You carve out 30 minutes, but then you spend 10 of them checking email or thinking about what to make for dinner. The trouble isn't necessarily the amount of time available—it's the quality of attention. Deep reading requires a special kind of time, one that can't be obtained merely by becoming more efficient. It demands a willingness to ignore the urgent in favor of the important.

This is where the "empty bottles" metaphor comes in. We feel a pressure to fill these different-sized bottles (days, hours, minutes) as they pass, lest we waste a single drop. If we don't fill them with productivity, we feel guilty. But reading, especially deep reading, requires us to let go of that guilt. It requires us to accept that some time is spent simply being, not doing.

Gary Eberle, author of *Sacred Time*, suggests scheduling regular times for reading. "Schedule it the way you would a doctor's appointment," he says. This ritualistic behavior helps us step out of the flow of daily life and into what Eberle calls "soul time." It allows us to achieve immersive reading, where we lose track of time and self.

Another trick is to always carry a book with you. But this only works if reading becomes your primary business of the day. You dip into it whenever you have a moment, surfacing only to deal with the urgent matters of life. If reading is just a backup plan for when nothing else is happening, it will never happen.

Ultimately, finding time to read is about prioritizing. It's about deciding that books matter more than the endless stream of updates, emails, and chores. It's a choice we make every day.

**How to Stay Happy**

(41) **Express your emotions.**
Children cry when they are sad and shout when they are angry. They don't suppress their feelings. Adults, however, often bottle up their emotions, which can lead to stress and unhappiness. To stay happy, allow yourself to feel and express what you are going through.

(42) **Be easily pleased.**
A child can be overjoyed by a simple, cheap T-shirt or a small toy. They find joy in the little things. As we grow up, we often demand more to feel satisfied. Relearning how to appreciate small gestures and simple pleasures can significantly boost our happiness levels.

(43) **Be silly.**
Children laugh constantly, often at nothing in particular. They indulge in silliness without fear of judgment. Adults tend to take themselves too seriously. Incorporating a bit of playfulness and silliness into your daily routine can reduce stress and lighten your mood.

(44) **Have fun.**
Make time for activities you love, whether it's playing sports, hanging out with friends, or pursuing a hobby. Schedule fun just like you schedule work. Prioritizing enjoyment is crucial for maintaining a balanced and happy life.

(45) **Don't overthink it.**
Sometimes, trying too hard to be happy can have the opposite effect. As the ancient Chinese philosopher Chuang Tzu suggested, happiness is often the absence of striving for it. Stop analyzing every moment and just let life happen.
