#### Text 1 (欧洲食品浪费)

Europe is not a food-surplus continent; it imports much of its animal feed. Yet it wastes a staggering amount of food. According to the UN Food and Agriculture Organization, Europeans throw away 89 million tonnes of food a year, enough to feed the hungry of the world twice over.

The reasons for this waste are complex. Supermarkets, obsessed with cosmetic perfection, reject tons of edible fruit and vegetables that are slightly misshapen or discolored. Consumers, confused by a maze of "sell-by," "use-by," and "best-before" dates, bin food that is still perfectly safe to eat. Restaurants serve portions that are too large, and households buy more than they can consume.

The economic cost is huge, but the environmental impact is even worse. Wasted food represents a waste of all the resources used to produce it: water, land, energy, and labor. Furthermore, when food rots in landfills, it generates methane, a potent greenhouse gas.

Solving this problem requires a multi-faceted approach. Governments can clarify labeling laws and offer tax incentives for businesses that donate unsold food to charities. Supermarkets can relax their cosmetic standards and sell "ugly" produce at a discount. Consumers can be educated to plan meals better and understand date labels.

Some countries are leading the way. France has banned supermarkets from throwing away unsold food, forcing them to donate it. Italy has introduced similar measures, simplifying the bureaucracy for food donation. These initiatives show that political will can make a significant difference.

If Europe can halve its food waste by 2030, as the UN suggests, it would save billions of euros and reduce its carbon footprint significantly. It is a goal that is both achievable and necessary.

#### Text 2 (明星育儿观的误导)

It's no surprise that Jennifer Senior's new book, *All Joy and No Fun*, is causing a stir. Her thesis is simple: parenting makes people miserable. She argues that while children bring joy, they also bring stress, exhaustion, and a loss of freedom.

Senior is not alone in this view. A growing number of studies suggest that parents are less happy than non-parents. The gap is particularly wide for single mothers and those with young children. This "parenting penalty" seems to be a modern phenomenon. In the past, children were economic assets; today, they are emotional projects that demand immense investment with uncertain returns.

However, this pessimistic view is challenged by others. Psychologist Sonja Lyubomirsky argues that the way we measure happiness matters. Most studies rely on self-reports of daily mood, which capture the stress of changing diapers but miss the deeper sense of meaning and fulfillment that parenting provides. "Happiness is not just about feeling good in the moment," she says. "It's also about having a purpose."

The media plays a significant role in shaping our perceptions. Celebrity magazines are filled with images of glowing, serene mothers who seem to balance career and family effortlessly. These unrealistic portrayals create pressure on ordinary parents to live up to an impossible standard. When real life falls short, parents feel inadequate and unhappy.

Perhaps the solution is not to stop having children, but to change our expectations. Parenting is hard work, but it can also be deeply rewarding if we accept the messiness and let go of the quest for perfection.

#### Text 3 (科学研究的不可靠性)

In 2011, a shocking study published in the journal *Nature* claimed that only 11% of landmark cancer research papers could be reproduced. This "replication crisis" has since spread to psychology, economics, and other fields. Scientists are increasingly worried that a large portion of published research is simply wrong.

Why is this happening? One major factor is the "publish or perish" culture. Researchers are under immense pressure to publish positive, novel results to secure funding and tenure. Negative results, which are equally important for scientific progress, are rarely published. This leads to a bias in the literature.

Another issue is the misuse of statistics. Many researchers engage in "p-hacking," where they manipulate data or analysis methods until they find a statistically significant result, even if it's a false positive. Small sample sizes and flexible research designs exacerbate the problem.

To address this crisis, the scientific community is taking steps. Some journals now require authors to preregister their study designs and analysis plans before collecting data. Others are encouraging the publication of replication studies and negative results. Open data policies are also being implemented to allow independent verification.

While these reforms are promising, changing the culture of science will take time. As one researcher noted, "We need to value truth over novelty." Only then can we restore trust in scientific findings.
