#### Text 2 (英国乡村保护)

The British countryside is under threat. Pressure from housing developers, infrastructure projects, and intensive farming is eroding the natural landscape that has inspired poets and painters for centuries. Campaigners argue that once lost, this heritage cannot be replaced.

However, others contend that the countryside must evolve to meet modern needs. Britain faces a housing crisis, and building on green belt land is seen by some as a necessary solution. They argue that protecting every acre of green space is elitist and ignores the needs of the growing population.

The debate often centers on the definition of "green belt." Originally intended to prevent urban sprawl, these zones now sometimes contain inefficient agricultural land or even golf courses, while cities choke on congestion. Reformers suggest a more nuanced approach: protect truly valuable natural habitats, but allow sensible development on low-value land.

Public opinion is divided. While most people say they value the countryside, few are willing to sacrifice their own garden for public housing. This "NIMBY" (Not In My Back Yard) mentality complicates planning decisions.

Finding a balance is crucial. The countryside should not be a museum frozen in time, nor should it be a free-for-all for developers. Sustainable planning that respects both nature and human needs is the only way forward.

#### Text 3 (CEO 跳槽潮)

For decades, the traditional career path for CEOs was to climb the ladder within a single company. Loyalty was rewarded, and stability was the norm. However, in recent years, a significant shift has occurred. More CEOs are jumping ship, moving from one corporation to another in search of better opportunities.

This trend, known as "CEO turnover," is driven by several factors. Boards of directors are increasingly willing to hire outsiders who bring fresh perspectives and proven track records. At the same time, successful executives are more confident in their marketability and less tied to a single organization.

The implications for companies are mixed. On one hand, new leaders can drive innovation and shake up stagnant cultures. On the other hand, high turnover can lead to instability and a lack of long-term vision. Employees may feel unsettled by frequent changes at the top.

Furthermore, the compensation packages for these moving CEOs have skyrocketed, fueling public debate about income inequality. Critics argue that this "mercenary" culture prioritizes short-term gains over the long-term health of the company.

Despite the criticism, the trend shows no sign of slowing down. As the business environment becomes more volatile, the demand for adaptable, experienced leaders who can hit the ground running continues to grow.

#### Text 4 (美国邮政 USPS 危机)

The United States Postal Service (USPS) is in trouble. Burdened by massive debt and declining mail volume, the agency faces an uncertain future. Once the backbone of American communication, it now struggles to compete with email and private courier services.

The financial woes of the USPS are largely structural. A 2006 law requires the agency to pre-fund retiree health benefits decades in advance, a burden no other government entity or private company faces. This mandate alone accounts for billions of dollars in annual losses.

Congress has debated various solutions, from cutting delivery days to privatizing parts of the service. However, political gridlock has prevented any major reform. Meanwhile, the USPS continues to lose money, raising concerns about its ability to fulfill its universal service obligation.

Despite these challenges, the USPS remains vital for many Americans, especially those in rural areas who rely on it for medicine, ballots, and packages. E-commerce has provided a glimmer of hope, as package delivery volumes have risen. But this revenue is not yet enough to offset the losses from first-class mail.

Without legislative action, the USPS may face a collapse that would disrupt the nation's economy and democracy. The time to act is now.
