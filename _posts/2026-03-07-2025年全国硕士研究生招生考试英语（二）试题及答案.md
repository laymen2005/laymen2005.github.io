#### Text 3 (适应性住房与老龄化)

Traditional homes are often unsuitable for the elderly, featuring stairs, narrow doorways, and slippery bathrooms. This mismatch leads to accidents, isolation, and premature moves to care facilities. Adaptive housing incorporates universal design principles from the start. Features include zero-step entrances, wider corridors for wheelchairs, and smart home technologies that monitor health and detect falls. Walls can be reconfigured, and kitchens adjusted in height, allowing the same house to serve a family for decades, from childhood to old age.

Governments are beginning to incentivize this shift. Tax breaks and grants are available for developers who include accessibility features in new builds, and for homeowners who retrofit existing properties. The economic argument is strong: keeping seniors in their homes longer reduces the burden on public healthcare and nursing systems.

Despite the benefits, adoption is slow. Cultural stigma associates adaptive features with "disability" rather than "longevity," leading many to resist modifications until a crisis occurs. Changing this mindset is crucial. Promoting adaptive housing as a smart, future-proof investment for all ages could accelerate its acceptance and improve quality of life for millions.
